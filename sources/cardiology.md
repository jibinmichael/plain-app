# Acute myocardial infarction

## Presentation

Acute myocardial infarction classically presents with central crushing chest pain radiating to the left arm or jaw, accompanied by sweating and nausea. The pain lasts more than 20 minutes and is not relieved by rest, which distinguishes it from stable angina.

## Investigations

An ECG should be performed first. ST elevation in leads II, III and aVF points to an inferior myocardial infarction, because this territory is supplied by the right coronary artery.

Troponin is the most specific biomarker of myocardial injury. It rises within 3 to 4 hours of the onset of chest pain and peaks at around 24 hours.

## Management

Primary percutaneous coronary intervention performed within 90 minutes is the first-line reperfusion strategy. Aspirin 300 mg should be given immediately as soon as the diagnosis is suspected.

Cardiogenic shock is the most dangerous early complication and must not be missed.

## Atrial fibrillation

In atrial fibrillation, the CHA2DS2-VASc score estimates stroke risk and guides the decision to start anticoagulation.
